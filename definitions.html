Personal Finance and Investing Dictionary

There are a lot of people who want to make investing sound complicated. Whether it's employers who benefit from employees not negotiating their benefits, insurance salesmen who want to convince you that buying their products is worth sacrificing future investments, or creators on the internet who promise you that you will become rich overnight if you just pay them for their advice.
But it does not need to be complicated. And hopefully this list of terms can help you understand many of the terms and acronyms that you may come across as you continue to learn more.

Dictionary:

Term Definition
Index Fund A portfolio of stocks or bonds designed to mimic the composition and perfamce of a financial market index.
APY (Annual Percentage Yield) The actual rate of return that will be earned in one year if the interest is compounded.
Roth IRA A special individual retirement account in the US where you pay taxes on money going into your account and all future withdrawls are tax free. There is a $6,000 contribution limit yearly and contributions can be withdrawn tax free while earnings can be withdrawn after 59 1/2 except under certain conditions.
401(k) A company-sponsored retirement account to which employees can contribute income, while employers may match contributions. Traditional 401(k)'s are pre-tax which means taxable income is reduced and withdrawls are taxed
Credit Score A credit score is a number between 300 and 850 that depicts a consumer's creditworthiness. A credit score plays a role in a lender's decision to offer credit.The score is affected by repayment history, length of credit history and total debt.
Interest Rate The interest rate is the amount charged on top of the principal by a lender to a borrower for the use of assets. Credit card debt will typically incur high interest rates.
Diversification A strategy that mizes a wide variety of investments within a portfolio. Diversification can occur across and within asset classes and also geographically by investing in both foreign and domestic markets.
Bull Market A bull market is a period of time in financial markets when the price of an asset or security rises continuously The commonly accepted definition of a bull market is when stock prices rise by 20% after two declines of 20% each.
Bear Market Bear markets occur when prices in a market decline by more than 20%, often accompanied by negative investor sentiment and declining economic prospects.
** Disclaimer: All definitions have been taken from investopedia.com. Please visit their site for more education on investing.
